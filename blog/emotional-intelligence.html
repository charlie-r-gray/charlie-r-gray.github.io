Emotional Intelligence

An exploration of the many intelligences

The two families of intelligence

There are many different types of intelligences. Many of them we give casual names: 'book smart' for those who have retained a lot of information, 'MacGyver' if you're adaptable at applying knowledge abstractly, 'quick learner' for those who absorb information with minimal exposure, 'street smart' for people who make wise choices under pressure, 'cunning' for those who can navigate around (or manipulate) other people, and more.

These many intelligences are distinct and not necessarily equal within a given person - not everybody who knows a lot can necessarily apply a lot, for example - but can be typically grouped into either of two 'families' of intelligence: EQ and IQ.

EQ: 'Emotional Quotient'

EQ is a measurement of our emotional intelligence - our understanding of relationships, people, and their psychological needs. EQ measures our ability to recognise and understand emotion - both our own, and others'. Like logical intelligence, this can be used both positively and negatively.

Different expressions of emotional intelligence include: street smarts, cunning, likability, subtlety, compassion, consideration, insight, being supportive, 'schmoozing', persuasion, PR, soothing, empathy, bereavement, guilt, duty, confidence, teamwork, leadership, determination, and noticing others' change in mood or emotion (such as when your parent or partner has silently become tense or irritable).

IQ: 'Intellectual Quotient'

IQ, by comparison, is a measurement of our logical intelligence - our understanding of patterns, systems, and physical limitations. IQ measures our ability to think abstractly: predict sequences, retain general knowledge, make connections between data, and estimate possible outcomes of certain conditions.

Different expressions of logical intelligence include: being book smart, a fast learner, improvisation, working memory, information retention, improvising solutions, curiosity, reading comprehension, problem-solving, design, and understanding complex abstract concepts (such as mathematics or astrophysics).

The relationship between EQ and IQ

It is a limitation of our language that we use 'intelligence' as a stand-alone word to refer primarily to one singular type of understanding and comprehension - that of patterns and logical concepts. The truth is that both families of intelligence - emotional, and logical - require nuanced and abstract thinking. They are both about understanding.

A given person may be excellent with calculating numbers, yet fail to recognise how the comments they make insult and alienate the people around them. Not merely because this person specifically wants to be left alone, a social pariah - but because they genuinely don't understand why they are, even as they estimate the trajectory of their homemade trebuchet.

IQ and EQ in the workplace

It is unfortunate that those who celebrate their IQ have pushed to a cultural idea that EQ is irrelevant or inferior - ironically while requiring and desiring many of the things inspired by and dependant upon it, such as teamwork in the workplace. Much as many citizens see fit to mistreat and look down upon the same truckers that deliver their goods, the same sanitation workers that empty their bins, the same staff that serve their food - yet would inevitably suffer and complain without them. The conceptual abstract has claimed prestige over their own indispensable foundation.

Familiar with the phrase, "it's not what you know: it's who you know?" Our IQ undeniably affects our success and productivity in our fields, but it is our EQ that determines where we can go, how high we can climb, how fast. Sycophancy may carry the worker further than correcting bosses' sloppy mistakes.

A world based on all IQ would be a meritocracy, but our world maintains social hierarchy, and rewards hard work with higher quotas. A world based on all EQ would be steeped in nepotism, creating disasters as unqualified friends and family adopt powerful positions. Our world often favours EQ, but has always relied on both EQ and IQ - we succeed when we value developing both.

Emotion and EQ in our personal lives

Our IQ's strength is in general logic. People, however, are much more nuanced than any pattern - and unfortunately, the nature of modern society and it's priority of production has been to mistreat and neglect emotion. When compared to our Thinking Selves, our Emotional Selves are considered weak, irrelevant, inferior. "Emotion is valid, but logic is better."

Emotion can be seen as almost dirty and inconvenient. Humans are 'higher beings'; we treat our ability to think abstractly as a divine, ascendant self, and our emotions as a hold-over from an fraught animal self - one that a truly self-aware person should aspire to ignore. That if ever a person makes a decision in emotion and 'want', and not in logic and 'should' - that they must be weak and slovenly; that a truly worthy person is capable of denying their emotion not just as needed, but all the time.

But this attitude is both incorrect and harmful. Humans are, at their core, emotional. Our emotion communicates our needs and helps us meet them.

Why humans ARE emotion

Without emotion, we can't establish values - what is important to us? What inspires our passion, love, fear? Do the needy bring us to feel grief? Does injustice inspire anger? Does obsolescence instill terror? And without values, we struggle to make well-reasoned decisions. Why is any choice more valuable than another, if you feel nothing for their outcome? Why do we push ourselves to be more, if there isn't something we desire to become? Something we fear and seek to escape?

Brain scans measuring conductively show that humans actually make their choices impulsively, seconds before the conscious self 'thinks' that they do; that their logical self is extrapolating and rationalising what is ultimately an emotional decision. Logic informs us as emotional creatures.

Ironically, the people who consider themselves to be the most 'rational' are are leaning upon their own emotionally-defined values to determine what they believe 'rational' even looks like! If you feel like you easily make logical decisions, that you don't struggle with making good choices - that is a sign that your Thinking Self has gotten good at telling your Emotional Self what it already wants to hear. Convincing yourself the compelling choice that feels right, always is right.

What this means for us?

It is a mistake to think that emotion is something we should 'rise above'. Emotion is not our weakness - it is the core of our relationships, teamwork, leadership, understanding. It is fundamental to our success, both as peers, and in the workplace. Our attitude towards, and management of, emotion is where our concern should lie. We should seek to recognise, understand and resolve emotion - not deny it.

A smart and educated person will struggle to succeed if they cannot interact with peers in a healthy way. A driven person can exhaust and hurt themselves if they don't take care of their own emotional needs. With emotional intelligence, we can find ourselves with help and support, positive relationships that nonetheless contribute to our success.

We can have productive lives without a healthy relationship with emotion, but we cannot have fulfilling ones.
