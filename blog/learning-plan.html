A learning plan

Where do we go from here?

Looking to the future

I know better than to try and predict the future at this point! But I'm generally adaptable (with notice, at least), and a fast learner. Driven by a desire to know many things, often for the sake of knowing them - I'd be capable of doing almost anything, so long as the work was varied enough and I was always encouraged to be doing more. Coding is incredibly broad, and yet, even deeper still. There is no point in code where you can lean back and say, 'okay, I'm done. Now what?'

Strengths and weaknesses in learning

I went into this a bit on my identities page, but in short: I have two modes. I either work incredibly hard to the point that my well-being suffers, or I struggle to work at all to the point that my work suffers. And, fun fact: the reason for the unsustainable first thing, is to make up for the inevitable second thing.

It's a vicious cycle, as each mode inspires and creates the other. I have to work twice as hard so that I can get half as far, and yet, a constant 'failure to meet up to my potential': including what I set for myself. For years I was convinced I was incapable of succeeding; that since I was always insufficient, the effort wasn't worth it. (In psychology, this is called 'learned helplessness'.)

That's not because I think that's the ideal, though. In fact, I'm here because I've learned otherwise. It's that for a long, long time, I'd not experienced an environment where looking after yourself first was just... okay. Both home life and school life always put responsibilities before relaxation, no matter your condition. Relaxation was a luxury, not a mental nor emotional need.

Though my environment has improved, our childhood lessons are hard to unlearn. To this day, I can't really 'relax' with work to do. I just feel guilty. I may exhaust myself trying to get work done, just so I can play a game or watch a TV show without feeling like a $%^&y person.

I'm glad that our world is waking up to how harmful that attitude really is. Emotional needs a finally being recognised as just that: needs. People are pushing for better work-life balance, that mental health is as valid of needing sick days as physical health is.

EDA is my first experience with an education institution that really observes and cares about the mental wellness of its faculty and students, and particularly: considers that wellness to be more important than their output.

Developing core skills with EDA

At this point, my enemy is myself. I know much of what a healthy person should look like; I just struggle to grow into it. My greatest struggle now is from emotional scars. Scars can fade, but they don't heal. And there certainly isn't much one can do to absolve me of my internal monologue.

...you know, it just now strikes me that one of my motivations for even joining EDA may not be healthy, either. I said that my goal was to prove myself - but I'll never feel satisfied that I've proven anything. To see how strong I've become - but I'll never feel anything other than not strong enough.

I've said before that what I really needed was a 'win': to have one thing I could look at, and feel satisfied. That my life has been nothing but failures, and my self-esteem could finally be fixed, if I just WON!

But... that's not right, is it? Because the truth is that I do have wins (or what should be, at least). A lot of them! And yet, I always have some reason why they don't 'count': because they don't fit the narrow definition of what I thought a 'win' should be. Or that I should have done more, that it could have been better, 'if only I wasn't', etc.

Even the kind words and compliments I receive can feel hollow, perfunctory, performative. Because if they actually knew me, they'd know I didn't deserve praise, right? They'd know that I had a 'potential' that I still wasn't living up to.

I'm sure the core skills of EDA will still teach me something. Perhaps something I'd never really considered or tried. It's one of the aspects of EDA I'm most curious about.

Managing my workload

Time management and schedule

I have a hard time scheduling myself. Mostly because I'm time-blind, so I can't ever meet the time constraints evenly: I estimate how long something should take, add 50%, and that's usually more correct. Then I feel like a failure for not meeting the time constraints - so rather than beat myself up for being so terrible at time-keeping, I should just accept that stuff takes the times that it takes. It's very easy for me to feel guilty for not doing enough. Measuring how guilty I should feel with a clock, is a risky play.

I want to set a (loose, forgiving, kind) schedule of [9am-12am], [2pm-5pm], [7pm-10pm]. Three blocks of three hours each, with two hours breaks. I've made a note that I can take breaks during (if needed - though I don't assume they'd be restful), but they be a max of 30min and that I have to work for at least 15min between them. I have a visual timer that can help me with the 30/15min part.

Working constructively and productively with others at EDA

Not quite sure what this question is asking? But I take my responsibilities to other people very seriously. I'm certainly capable of working alongside other people.

Do note though that my auditory-processing (hearing) is impaired; if I'm deeply focused on something, you'll need to actually get my attention before you talk to me. It's not that I don't care, it's that by the time I realised you were speaking, I missed the first half. Also, if I ask you to repeat yourself, it is far more helpful to speak more clearly (e-nun-ci-ate) rather than necessarily louder, but you may still need to be louder than background noise.

What you will do when things are building up and starting to get to you?

I have a few things I do to reduce stress, both preventative and responsive (in no particular order):

  • noise machines
  • music
  • touching soft things
  • take a nap
  • eat a treat
  • meditation exercises
  • mindfulness exercises
  • self-abuse self-talk
  • being outside (Green Time)
If that really isn't enough, I'll probably have to admit that I need some leniency and talk to somebody about it.

A commitment as to how and where you will seek help in a timely way

Help like... emotionally? For burnout? Or for being stuck?

My problems are, first and foremost, mine. They are mine to solve, and it is my responsibility to do what I can by myself, first. Asking for help anytime I got stuck or frustrated would never see either of us do anything else. Everybody else has their own work to do, too.

However, I would like to think that if I reached the point that what I was trying for myself wasn't working, that I would be able to admit that I wasn't up to it and I needed support; rather than silently letting things crumble around me. I've already lost enough because I was too ashamed of emotional needs to admit that I needed help - but now I'm more ashamed that I failed because I didn't ask.

Asking for help for emotional needs ('I'm not okay') is still much harder for me than asking for help for practical ones ('how do I?'). The latter is a reasonable logistical problem that anybody learning has, and can't ever be shameful. But the former is a reflection of my ability and worth. It makes me feel weak and pitiable. And I do still feel ashamed for needing that.

What I expect from the facilitation team

Not sure. I don't know what I can expect. I think what I'd value the most is getting good, constructive feedback. Especially if responsive and timely.

It's probably helpful for you to acknowledge the things I have learned and done well (because I'm not good at noticing that, myself), but for me to believe it, it can't sound like flattery. So... tricky tightrope to walk. I don't envy you.
